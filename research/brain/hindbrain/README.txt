# Hindbrain

Major subparts:
- pons
- medulla oblongata
- reticular formation
- cerebellum (sometimes grouped here)

Subparts of pons:
	- pontine nuclei
	- cranial nerve nuclei

Subparts of medulla oblongata:
	- pyramids
	- olives
	- cranial nerve nuclei

Subparts of reticular formation:
	- ascending reticular activating system (ARAS)
	- descending reticulospinal tracts

Functionality:
The hindbrain controls vital autonomic functions (breathing, heart rate), basic motor patterns, balance, and arousal. It acts as a bridge between the spinal cord and higher brain regions, and is essential for survival.


What affects it:
- Sensory input from the body (e.g., proprioception, pain)
- Signals from the cortex and midbrain
- Neurotransmitters (e.g., serotonin, norepinephrine)
- Damage (stroke, trauma) can be life-threatening


How it affects other parts:
- Regulates arousal and sleep-wake cycles via the reticular formation
- Relays sensory and motor information between the brain and spinal cord
- Modulates reflexes and basic movement patterns


Explanation:
- The hindbrain, or rhombencephalon, is the posterior part of the brain, comprising the medulla oblongata, pons, and sometimes the cerebellum. It develops from the rhombencephalon during embryogenesis and serves as a critical interface between the spinal cord and higher brain regions. The medulla oblongata controls vital autonomic functions like respiration, heart rate, and blood pressure through nuclei such as the respiratory center and cardiovascular center. The pons contains pontine nuclei that relay information between the cerebral cortex and cerebellum, and houses cranial nerve nuclei for facial and auditory functions. The reticular formation, a diffuse network throughout the hindbrain, regulates arousal, sleep-wake cycles, and attention via the ascending reticular activating system (ARAS). This region's compact structure ensures rapid, automatic responses essential for survival, with damage often causing severe deficits or death.
